Finance 101 — The Basics

Never bought a stock? That's fine. Here's everything you need to understand the strategies in these videos, explained without jargon.

Core Vocabulary
Brokerage
The middleman between you and the stock market. Old brokerages = calling a person on the phone. Modern ones = an app (Robinhood, Fidelity). Alpaca is a brokerage with an API, so Claude can connect and trade for you via code.
API
Application Programming Interface — a way for software to talk to other software. Alpaca's API lets Claude place buy/sell orders without any human clicking a button. Think of it as a drive-through window for code.
Paper Trading
Simulated trading with fake money. Same real prices, same real stocks, but no real money changes hands. It's the safest way to test a strategy before going live.
Stock / Share
Buying a share means you own a tiny piece of a company. If the company grows in value, your share does too. If it shrinks, so does your share. You can sell at any time (when markets are open).
Market Price
The current price a stock is selling for right now. It changes every second during market hours (9:30 AM – 4:00 PM EST, Mon–Fri).
Stop Loss
A rule: "If this stock drops to $X, sell it automatically." It limits how much you can lose on a trade. Without one, a dropping stock could wipe you out while you're not watching.
Trailing Stop
A smarter stop loss. Instead of a fixed price, it follows the stock upward. If the stock rises, the stop rises with it. If the stock then falls back, the stop triggers — and you keep most of your gains.
Ladder Buy (DCA)
Buying more shares as the price drops, in stages. If you buy at $100, $80, and $60, your average cost is $80. This is called Dollar Cost Averaging. You're getting a better deal on each dip instead of just losing money.
Whales
Institutional investors (hedge funds, banks, billionaires) who move millions or billions of dollars at once. When a whale buys a stock, the price often moves. Tracking whale activity is a real strategy called "smart money" following.
S&P 500
An index of the 500 largest US companies. Often used as a benchmark: "Did my strategy beat the S&P?" If it did, you outperformed most actively managed funds. +15% in a year is typical for the S&P; beating it consistently is rare.
MCP (Model Context Protocol)
A way to plug Claude into external services (data feeds, apps, brokerages) so it can both read and act on live information. Think of it as a power outlet: the electricity (data) is already in the walls — MCP is how Claude plugs in.
Cron Job / Schedule
A recurring automated task. Claude can set itself a cron job: "Check Tesla's price every 5 minutes during market hours." You don't have to touch it — your computer runs it while you're doing other things.
Options — Explained From Zero

Options are contracts that give you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell a stock at a set price before a set date. Think of it exactly like insurance.

🚗 CAR INSURANCE
You pay $100/month → You get the right to file a claim if you crash

If nothing happens → insurer keeps your $100
If you crash → insurer covers the damage
≡
📈 STOCK OPTION
You pay a premium → You get the right to buy/sell at a locked price

If stock doesn't move → seller keeps your premium
If stock moves your way → you profit
Call Option (buying)
Right to buy a stock at a locked price. Apple is at $200; you buy a call at $210 strike. If Apple hits $230, you can buy at $210 and pocket $20 difference. If it doesn't hit $210 — contract expires, you lose the premium.
Put Option (buying)
Right to sell a stock at a locked price. You own Apple at $200; buy a put at $190. If Apple drops to $170, you can still sell at $190. The put is protection — insurance against a price drop.
Strike Price
The locked price in the contract. If you buy a call with a $210 strike on Apple, you can buy at $210 regardless of where the stock actually trades. The further away the strike from the current price, the cheaper (and riskier) the option.
Premium
The price you pay for the option contract. If you buy one contract (typically = 100 shares) at $5 per share, your premium is $500. This is your max loss as a buyer. As a seller, this is what you collect upfront.
Expiration Date
Options don't last forever. After this date, the contract dies. If your conditions weren't met, the buyer loses the premium and the seller keeps it. The wheel strategy typically uses 2–4 week expirations.
Selling Options (being the insurer)
You flip the table: instead of paying a premium, you collect it. Someone pays you for the contract. If the stock doesn't hit the strike price by expiration, you keep the money and do it again. Insurance companies make billions this way.
Getting Assigned
When you've sold a put and the stock falls below your strike price — the buyer exercises the contract and you're forced to buy the shares at that price. In the wheel strategy, this is expected and planned for, not a disaster.
Covered Call
You sell someone the right to buy your shares at a higher price. "Covered" means you already own the shares, so if they're called away you just hand them over. You collect a premium either way.
Pine Script & TradingView Terms
TradingView
A popular charting platform used by millions of traders. Shows price action, indicators, volume, and more for stocks, crypto, and other assets. Has a desktop app and a browser version.
Indicator
A mathematical calculation plotted on your chart to help identify patterns. Examples: moving averages (EMA, SMA), RSI (momentum), MACD (trend). They don't predict the future — they describe what's happening.
Pine Script
TradingView's own coding language for creating custom indicators and strategy backtests. Claude can write Pine Script from your plain-English description and install it directly onto your chart.
Backtesting
Running a strategy against historical data to see how it would have performed in the past. Not a guarantee of future results, but a sanity check before trusting a strategy with real money.
EMA (Exponential Moving Average)
An average of a stock's price over N periods (e.g., 21-day, 50-day, 200-day), weighted to emphasize recent prices more. When price is above the 200 EMA, the trend is generally considered bullish.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
A momentum indicator from 0–100. Above 70 = potentially overbought (might drop). Below 30 = potentially oversold (might bounce). In the example strategy: RSI 35–58 is the "buy zone."
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence — a trend-following indicator. Traders use it to spot potential reversals or confirm a trend is still in force. Used in the example Van Deop + Tone Vase strategy.
CDP (Chrome DevTools Protocol)
A technical protocol that lets external programs read and control browser windows at the code level. The TradingView MCP uses CDP to let Claude read live chart data — not a screenshot, but actual code values.

The Three Strategies

Each strategy encodes a specific set of rules. Claude doesn't improvise — it executes what you define. The smarter the rules, the smarter the bot.

Strategy 1 — Trailing Stop Bot

Like a smart thermostat: set the rules once, and it manages temperature (price) automatically all day. The floor only goes up, never down.

🛒
Buy Entry
Claude buys N shares at market price. You specify the stock (e.g., Tesla) and quantity. All at once, or in stages.
↓
🔒
Set Initial Floor (Stop Loss)
Claude sets a stop loss at -10% below purchase price. If the stock drops that far, sell everything. Max loss is fixed.
↓
📈
Stock Rises → Move the Floor Up
Every time the stock gains +10%, Claude raises the stop loss floor to 5% below the new high. The floor only moves up. You can never fall back to your original risk level.
↓
🪜
Stock Drops → Ladder Buy
If the stock dips -20% → buy 20 more shares. If -30% → buy 10 more. Buying the dip lowers your average cost, so a smaller recovery puts you back in profit.
↓
🔄
Floor Is Hit → Sell + Reset
If the stock falls to the trailing stop, Claude sells. You've limited your loss. Capital is freed up. Claude looks for the next opportunity — same stock at a better price, or something else entirely.

the current price every time it rises 10%. Ladder buy: if -20% drop, buy
20 more shares. If -30%, buy 10 more. Schedule a check every 5 min,
Mon–Fri 9am–4pm. Show me a summary after each action.
Claude will set a cron job automatically using /schedule. In the Claude Desktop sidebar, click the clock icon → Scheduled to see active tasks.

Strategy 3 — The Wheel (Options Income)

You become the insurance company. You collect premiums at every stage. The stock can go up, down, or sideways — you're earning income regardless. This is why management is the hard part, and why Claude makes it accessible.

💰
Stage 1 — Sell a Cash-Secured Put
Pick a stock you want to own. Sell a put at ~10% below current price, expiring 2–4 weeks out. Collect the premium immediately. You're promising: "I'll buy this stock at $X if it drops there."
↓
⤷
Outcome A — Put Expires Worthless
Stock stayed above your strike. You keep the premium. Nobody makes you buy anything. Go back to Stage 1. Sell another put. Collect another premium.
↓
⤷
Outcome B — You Get Assigned (Stock Drops)
Stock fell below your strike. You now own 100 shares — but at your strike price minus the premium you already collected. Your real cost basis is lower than anyone who bought it on the open market.
↓
📤
Stage 2 — Sell a Covered Call
Now you own the shares. Sell a call at ~10% above what you paid, 2–4 weeks out. You collect another premium. If the stock rises above that strike, your shares get "called away" (sold) at a profit. If it doesn't, you keep the shares and the premium.
↓
🔄
Shares Called Away → Repeat
Your shares were sold at a profit. You've collected: put premium + call premium + stock gain. Go back to Stage 1. The wheel keeps spinning.

✓ Wheel Strategy Rules (hardcoded into bot)

Never sell a put without enough cash to buy the shares if assigned. Never sell a covered call below your cost basis. Track premiums across all cycles. Close any contract early if it hits 50% profit before expiration. Daily summary at market close. Do nothing outside market hours.

Both videos contain important nuances that are easy to miss on a first watch. Read this before you start building anything real.

✓ Do This

✅Set rules before you let Claude trade. Giving AI a pile of money and saying "figure it out" is the worst thing you can do.
✅Use separate paper accounts for each strategy so you can track what's actually working.
✅Roleplay scenarios with Claude — ask "what would you do if Tesla drops to $100?" before it happens.
✅Save your API credentials to a file in the trading folder so Claude doesn't lose them between sessions.
✅For the wheel strategy: always verify Claude has enough cash in the account to cover a put assignment before selling.
✅For TradingView MCP: download the desktop app, not the browser version — the CDP connection requires it.
✅Use the improved "one-shot setup" prompt (LewisWJackson's fork) rather than the manual install — it handles all edge cases.
✗ Avoid This
❌Don't treat this as financial advice. Neither creator is a financial advisor — they're showing you how to build cool things with AI.
❌Don't use real money until you've run the strategy on paper for a sustained period and understand why every rule is there.
❌Don't share your API secret key. Anyone with it can place trades on your account.
❌Don't expect political trade data to be real-time. Congress has up to 45 days to report. The strategy still works because they tend to hold positions long-term.
❌Don't skip the health check step (tv_health_check) for TradingView MCP — connecting without verifying leads to silent failures.
❌Don't screenshot charts and paste them into AI — that approach is outdated and imprecise. Use the MCP for live data.
❌Don't confuse backtesting results with guarantees. The McCaul +34.8% is a historical simulation — markets change.
❌Don't sell a covered call below your cost basis — this would guarantee a loss if your shares get called away.
Key Mechanics to Understand
⚠️
Cron jobs only run while your computer is on. Claude's scheduled tasks use your local machine. If you shut down, the bot stops. For true 24/7 automation you'd need a cloud server.
⚠️
Options require owning 100 shares per contract. One options contract represents 100 shares. If you sell a put at $230 on Tesla, you must have enough cash to buy 100 shares ($23,000) if assigned.
⚠️
The wheel strategy fails without discipline. Most people give up because managing positions week after week is tedious. That's exactly what Claude handles — but you still need to check in and verify it's running correctly.
⚠️
Ladder buys increase risk on losing trades. Buying more on the way down (DCA) lowers your average cost, but it also means you're putting more money into a losing position. Only do this with stocks you genuinely believe in long-term.
⚠️
Pine Script errors will happen. When Claude creates a Pine Script, TradingView may show errors. The MCP setup handles this — Claude can see the errors in the live code and will keep fixing them automatically until resolved.
⚠️
Claude Code vs Claude Desktop. The Alpaca trading bot uses Claude Desktop (with its file system and scheduling tools). The TradingView MCP uses Claude Code (terminal). These are different interfaces with different capabilities.
